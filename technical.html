Procedural Content Generation with LLMs

This project comes from an undergraduate research course with Professor Jim Whitehead, where I worked with a group of students on procedural content generation for games. Throughout the class, we explored noise-based generation, tile-based maps, and wave function collapse.

My primary contribution was working on developing the TinyTown generator, with a focus on structured the placement of decorative objects within the procedurally generated tilemap. I also worked on evaluating large language model outputs by analyzing and correcting responses related to tile identification and spatial reasoning.

As part of the research, we created the TinyTownQA dataset to study how well different language models understand and reason about tile-based game worlds. This work helped inform experiments comparing text-based, image-based, and hybrid world representations.

The results of this project were published at FDG 2025. Through this research group, I gained practical experience with procedural systems, collaborative research, and building tools to support experimental evaluation.
